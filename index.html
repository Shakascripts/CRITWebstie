What is the Impact of Tissue Engineering Technologies in Addressing the Global Organ Transplant Shortage?
Zaire-Zidane Quinell Bennett (620176561)  |  FOUN1014 · Stream 12  |  The University of the West Indies
Abstract
Over 100,000 patients await organ transplants in the US alone, yet traditional donation cannot meet demand. This poster examines three tissue engineering approaches — scaffold-based construction, 3D bioprinting, and early clinical translation — that are emerging as transformative solutions. Evidence shows meaningful progress across all three fronts, while vascularization and scalability remain the most critical barriers to widespread clinical use.

SIGNIFICANCE
Early clinical success demonstrates tissue engineering is no longer purely experimental — it is already improving patient outcomes.
Remaining Challenges
Organ Complexity vs. Engineering Readiness
Vascularization
Functional blood vessel networks must be embedded in engineered tissues — without adequate blood flow, larger organs cannot survive after implantation (Zhang et al., 2024)
The absence of a functional vascular network is the primary barrier to scaling tissues beyond a few hundred micrometres (Novosel et al., 2011)
Scalability
Laboratory success does not equal affordable, reliable mass production (Zoghi, 2024)
Complexity Gap
The gap between simple structures (ears) and complex vascularized organs (kidneys, hearts) remains substantial and requires sustained interdisciplinary collaboration
Conclusion
Scaffold-based approaches like decellularization and bioprinted patches have demonstrated that it is possible to create functional tissue structures using both natural and engineered frameworks.
3D bioprinting technologies are rapidly improving in precision and material quality, bringing on-demand organ fabrication closer to reality.
Early clinical successes — such as 3D-printed ear implants — confirm that these technologies are already making a tangible difference in patients' lives (Kim et al., 2024).
Significant challenges remain, particularly around vascularization and scalability (Zhang et al., 2024; Novosel et al., 2011).
With sustained investment and interdisciplinary collaboration, tissue engineering has the potential to reshape the treatment of organ failure and save countless lives.
